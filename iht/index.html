Planning Options

The scenarios

Below are the realistic paths forward. Each one shows what the IHT bill would be, and what's required to make it work. None of these are guarantees — they depend on timing and decisions that still need to be made.

Option A — Do nothing new

Keep things as they are

No new gifts, no new investments, no changes to the will structure. The estate is settled as-is when the time comes.

This is the simplest and lowest-effort path. There's no risk of things going wrong with planning — but equally, nothing is done to reduce the tax bill. The estate pays what it owes.

One option not yet on this list — marriage. If Dennis and Gitana were to marry, everything passing to Gitana would become IHT-exempt. The Reading flat (£350k) alone would be removed from the taxable estate, saving ~£140k in IHT and meaning Gitana receives the full £350k rather than ~£264k net. This isn't a planning decision to be made lightly, but it is the single largest IHT lever available — and it costs nothing financially.

Option C — Business Relief investment (2-year rule)

Invest in qualifying EIS/SEIS businesses — uses a 2-year window, not 7

Certain types of investment — including in qualifying private companies under the Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS) or Seed EIS (SEIS) — qualify for Business Relief. If held for 2 years, they fall completely outside the estate for IHT.

This is relevant because the 2-year window is realistic in a way that 7 years is not at 82.

Example: Dad invests £200k into qualifying EIS/SEIS companies. If he lives 2 years and still holds the shares, that £200k is IHT-free — saving £80,000 in tax.

Not yet explored for our situation: Whether your or your sister's businesses qualify for EIS or SEIS hasn't been looked into yet. This is one of the higher-priority things to check — if either qualifies, it's one of the few genuinely practical IHT-reduction routes within a 2-year window. An accountant can assess this without any money moving.

Option E — Life insurance written in trust

Cover the tax bill rather than trying to eliminate it

A whole-of-life policy — written into a trust — pays out a lump sum on death. Because it's held in trust, the payout goes directly to beneficiaries and doesn't form part of the estate.

The point isn't to avoid the IHT bill — it's to make sure the family doesn't have to sell the house or liquidate investments at short notice to pay HMRC (who require payment within 6 months).

At 82, premiums will be expensive. A smaller policy (£80–150k) covering part of the likely bill may be more realistic than full cover.

Option F — Charitable giving in the will

Leave 10% to charity, reduce the tax rate to 36%

If at least 10% of the net estate is left to a registered charity, HMRC reduces the IHT rate from 40% down to 36% on the remainder. This is a meaningful saving and leaves a lasting legacy.

Example: If £90k goes to charity (10% of £900k estate), the remaining taxable amount is taxed at 36% instead of 40%. IHT drops from ~£160k to ~£112k — a saving of ~£48k.

Common Questions

Things that keep coming up

Why not just put everything in a trust?

A trust doesn't avoid the 7-year rule — it just changes the type of transfer from a "potentially exempt transfer" (gift) to a "chargeable lifetime transfer." The clock still runs. If the estate is transferred into a trust and Dennis passes within 7 years, IHT is still due.

There's also an immediate 20% tax charge if more than £325k is placed into a trust in a single go. And trusts have ongoing 10-year anniversary charges (up to 6% of the trust's value every decade) and exit charges when money comes out.

Trusts are useful for control — making sure money is used the right way, or protecting assets for grandchildren. They're not a shortcut around inheritance tax, especially at 82.

If Dennis still lives in the Kings Road home, putting it into a trust while he remains there is a "gift with reservation of benefit" — legally, it stays in his estate regardless of what the paperwork says. He'd have to pay full market rent to the trust to change that, which isn't practical.

Why does Gitana pay so much IHT if she's not even a child?

Because Dennis and Gitana did not marry, there is no spouse exemption on anything she receives. In UK law, only a legal spouse or civil partner benefits from unlimited IHT exemption. Unmarried partners — however long-standing — are treated the same as any other beneficiary.

The Reading flat is worth ~£350k and passes entirely to Gitana. Under clause 7 of the will, she bears IHT in proportion to what she receives — roughly 54% of the estate, meaning roughly 54% of the ~£160k bill (~£86k). So her net inheritance from the flat is around £264k rather than £350k.

There are a few ways this could be addressed. If Dennis and Gitana were to marry, the spouse exemption would apply and Gitana's share would be entirely IHT-free — reducing the overall bill significantly and meaning more actually reaches her. Alternatively, the will could be restructured so her bequest is expressed net of IHT (meaning the estate absorbs more of the cost). Either option would require the solicitor who drafted the will to advise properly.

What happens to the pension when Dennis passes?

The pension bypasses the will entirely. It goes directly to whoever Dennis has named on the nomination form held by the pension provider — this could be Gitana, Alexia, Coris, or any combination. This is not automatic — it's a discretionary decision made by the pension trustees, but they almost always follow the nomination.

Under current rules (before April 2027): if he passes after age 75 (which is likely), beneficiaries pay income tax at their own rate when they draw from it — but there's no IHT at all.

After April 2027, the pension will count in the estate for IHT. Beneficiaries could face both IHT (40%) and income tax on drawdown — a combined rate of up to 64–67% in the worst case. That makes checking and updating the nomination form urgent before then.

Can Dennis give more money to reduce the estate further?

Yes — but the 7-year rule applies to all gifts over £3,000 per year (the annual exemption). Any amount given above that starts the 7-year clock. There's no limit on how much can be given, but the larger the gift, the more it needs time to clear the estate.

There's also a "gifts out of surplus income" exemption — if Dennis makes regular gifts from his income (not capital) and it doesn't affect his standard of living, those can be immediately exempt from IHT. This requires keeping good records and the pattern being demonstrably regular.

Does the £120k already given to Alexia reduce her share of the inheritance?

That's a family conversation, not a tax question. From HMRC's perspective, the gift was made and the 7-year clock is running. Whether the £120k is treated as an advance on Alexia's inheritance — or a separate gift on top of her share — is something the family needs to agree on. The will doesn't currently address this explicitly. It could be added as a clause, or handled informally if everyone agrees. The important thing is that everyone understands and is comfortable with the arrangement.

What does the family need to do when Dennis passes?

Gitana and Alexia are named as executors in the will. They apply for probate together. HMRC requires an IHT return showing all assets, gifts made in the past 7 years, and the allowances being claimed. IHT must be paid within 6 months of death (before probate is granted for most assets). Under clause 7, the IHT is borne proportionally — so Alexia and Coris together cover roughly 75% of the bill, Gitana around 25%.

Assets can't usually be sold until probate is granted, but the tax is due before that — so having cash available (or a life policy payout) is important.

What's Changing

Recent and upcoming law changes

The core allowances (£325k and £175k) haven't changed and are now frozen until 2031. But three things have changed or are changing that affect this plan.

The good news: The 7-year gift rule, Business Relief 2-year rule, and charitable giving 36% rate are all unchanged. The planning options above remain valid.

1. Pensions entering the IHT estate — April 2027

From 6 April 2027, unused defined contribution pension funds will count as part of the taxable estate. Currently the £300k pension sits entirely outside the calculation. After 2027, it's inside it.

This is in the draft Finance Bill 2025–26 and is expected to become law. It would add ~£120k to the IHT bill if no further planning is done. The most effective response is to make sure the nomination form is correct and, if appropriate, consider drawing pension income now and gifting from it (which can be immediately exempt if it's from regular surplus income).

2. AIM share relief halved — April 2026

Previously, investing in AIM-listed shares for 2 years gave 100% IHT relief. From April 2026, this drops to 50% relief — meaning an effective 20% IHT charge on those holdings. If any investments in AIM shares are being considered specifically for IHT purposes, the timing matters.

3. Thresholds frozen until 2031

The Nil Rate Band (£325k) has been frozen since 2009. It will now remain frozen until at least 2031. As property and asset values rise, more of the estate becomes taxable — even without any changes to the rules.

Bottom line: Nothing has changed that breaks the planning laid out above. But the April 2027 pension change is significant and worth acting around before then — specifically making sure the nomination form is up to date and considering whether drawing pension income now (and gifting it) makes sense.
